💡 1. Vital Verb Tenses

Although pure grammar questions are rare in SNBT, understanding tenses is crucial for reading comprehension. Embedded grammar errors are common in English Reading texts.

A. Simple Tenses

  • Present Simple: Habits, facts, general truths. "Water boils at 100°C." "They play tennis every Sunday."
  • Past Simple: Completed action at a specific time. "He arrived yesterday." "The war ended in 1945."
  • Future Simple: Predictions, decisions. "It will rain tomorrow." "I will help you."

B. Perfect Tenses (The SNBT Favorites)

  • Present Perfect (has/have + V3): Past action connected to present, or unspecified time. "She has finished her task." "I have lived here since 2010."
  • Past Perfect (had + V3): Action completed before another past action. "By the time he arrived, she had already left."
  • Future Perfect (will have + V3): Action that will be completed before a future time. "By 2030, we will have finished the project."
💡 Key Signal Words:
• Present Perfect: since, for, already, yet, ever, never, so far, recently
• Past Simple: yesterday, ago, last year, in 2020, when
• Past Perfect: by the time, before, after, already (with past context)

C. Continuous Tenses

  • Present Continuous (is/am/are + V-ing): Action happening now or temporary situations. "She is studying for SNBT."
  • Past Continuous (was/were + V-ing): Action in progress at a specific past time. "I was reading when the phone rang."
🛑 Stative Verbs Cannot Use Continuous: Some verbs never use -ing form: know, believe, understand, want, need, prefer, like, love, hate, own, belong, seem, appear, mean.
❌ "I am knowing the answer." → ✅ "I know the answer."

🧩 2. Subject-Verb Agreement

The verb must align with the subject's number (singular/plural). This is one of the most frequently tested grammar points in SNBT.

A. Basic Rules

  • Singular subject → singular verb (adds -s): "The student studies hard."
  • Plural subject → plural verb (no -s): "The students study hard."

B. Tricky Cases

  • Prepositional phrases between S and V: Ignore them! "The list of new applications is confusing." (Subject = "The list", not "applications")
  • Each, every, everyone, everybody: Always singular. "Each student has a book."
  • Neither...nor / Either...or: Verb agrees with the CLOSER subject. "Neither the teacher nor the students were present."
  • Uncountable nouns: Always singular. "The information is accurate." "Research shows that..."
  • Collective nouns: Usually singular in American English. "The team is winning."
🛑 SNBT Trap: Test makers will separate the subject and verb with LONG modifying phrases to confuse you!
"The list of new software applications developed by the research team at the university are confusing." → WRONG!
Subject = "The list" (Singular) → Should be: "The list [...] is confusing."
Practice: Identify the correct verb:
"The news about the economic reforms _____ encouraging."
A. are   B. is   C. were   D. have been
Answer: B. is ("news" is uncountable/singular)

🔬 3. Passive Voice vs Active

Passive voice is used heavily in scientific, formal, and academic texts — exactly the type of texts in SNBT.

Structure

Subject + Be + Past Participle (V3) + (by agent)

  • Active: "Alexander Fleming discovered penicillin in 1928."
  • Passive: "Penicillin was discovered (by Alexander Fleming) in 1928."

Passive in Different Tenses

  • Simple Present: is/am/are + V3. "English is spoken worldwide."
  • Simple Past: was/were + V3. "The building was constructed in 1999."
  • Present Perfect: has/have been + V3. "The report has been submitted."
  • Modal: modal + be + V3. "The data can be analyzed."
  • Continuous: is/was being + V3. "The bridge is being repaired."
💡 When is Passive Used?
• When the doer is unknown: "The window was broken."
• When the action is more important than who did it: "The experiment was conducted in 2022."
• In formal/academic writing: "It is believed that...", "It has been shown that..."

🔭 4. Conditional Sentences (If Clause)

Hypothetical situations and their conditions. Conditionals are heavily tested in SNBT because they require precise tense knowledge.

The Four Types

  • Type 0 (General Truth): If + Present, Present. "If you heat water to 100°C, it boils."
  • Type 1 (Real Future): If + Present, will + V1. "If it rains, I will stay home."
  • Type 2 (Unreal Present): If + Past, would + V1. "If I were rich, I would buy a house."
  • Type 3 (Unreal Past): If + Past Perfect, would have + V3. "If I had studied harder, I would have passed."
🛑 Critical Note: In Type 2, always use "were" (not "was") for all subjects, especially in formal writing:
✅ "If I were you, I would accept the offer."
❌ "If I was you..." (incorrect in formal writing)

Mixed Conditionals

Sometimes SNBT mixes Type 2 and Type 3 conditions:

Past condition, present result:
"If I had taken (Type 3) the medicine, I would be (Type 2) healthy now."

Present condition, past result:
"If she were (Type 2) more careful, she would not have made (Type 3) that mistake."

Wish Clauses (Related Pattern)

  • Present wish: I wish + Past Simple. "I wish I knew the answer." (but I don't know)
  • Past wish: I wish + Past Perfect. "I wish I had studied harder." (but I didn't)
  • Future wish: I wish + would + V1. "I wish it would stop raining."

🔗 5. Relative Clauses & Connectors

Relative clauses add extra information about a noun. Understanding them is crucial for parsing complex SNBT sentences.

Relative Pronouns

  • who/whom: for people. "The scientist who discovered DNA..."
  • which: for things/animals. "The book which I read..."
  • that: for people or things (restrictive clauses only). "The car that was stolen..."
  • whose: for possession. "The student whose project won..."
  • where: for places. "The city where I grew up..."
  • when: for times. "The year when we graduated..."
Defining (Restrictive): Essential information, no commas.
"Students who study hard will succeed." (Only those who study hard)

Non-defining (Non-restrictive): Extra information, with commas.
"My brother, who lives in Jakarta, is a doctor." (Extra info about my only brother)

Common Academic Connectors

  • Addition: moreover, furthermore, in addition, besides
  • Contrast: however, nevertheless, on the other hand, whereas, although
  • Cause/Effect: therefore, consequently, as a result, thus, hence
  • Example: for instance, for example, such as, namely
  • Concession: despite, in spite of, even though, although

🔮 6. Gerund vs Infinitive

Some verbs are followed by gerunds (-ing), some by infinitives (to + verb), and some by both with different meanings.

Verbs + Gerund Only

enjoy, avoid, consider, deny, finish, imagine, keep, mind, practice, suggest, risk, admit, delay, quit

✅ "I enjoy reading books."
❌ "I enjoy to read books."

Verbs + Infinitive Only

want, need, decide, hope, plan, agree, refuse, promise, offer, learn, expect, afford, manage, pretend

✅ "She decided to study abroad."
❌ "She decided studying abroad."

Verbs + Both (Different Meaning!)

  • remember + gerund = remember a past action. "I remember locking the door." (I locked it and I remember doing it)
  • remember + infinitive = remember to do a future action. "Remember to lock the door." (Don't forget to lock it)
  • stop + gerund = stop the activity. "He stopped smoking." (He quit smoking)
  • stop + infinitive = stop in order to do something. "He stopped to smoke." (He paused to have a cigarette)
  • try + gerund = experiment. "Try using a different method."
  • try + infinitive = attempt. "I tried to open the door."

🎯 7. Strategi Menjawab SNBT

  1. Watch out for Inversion. When negative adverbs begin a sentence, the verb precedes the subject! "Hardly had she left when it rained." "Never have I seen such beauty."
  2. Identify the Subject First. In complex sentences, always find the TRUE subject before checking the verb. Cross out prepositional phrases and relative clauses mentally.
  3. Check Tense Consistency. In SNBT reading passages, pay attention to tense shifts. If a paragraph is in past tense, a sudden shift to present tense should alert you.
  4. Look for Parallel Structure. Items in a list must be in the same grammatical form: "She likes swimming, hiking, and cycling" (all gerunds).
  5. Master "It" Constructions. Academic English frequently uses: "It is believed that...", "It has been suggested that...", "It is worth noting that..."
  6. Recognize Reduced Clauses. "The man (who was) sitting next to me..." — relative pronouns can be omitted, making sentences harder to parse.
💡 Quick Test: If you're unsure about a grammar point, try reading the sentence out loud. Your ear will often catch errors that your eyes miss!
